16S rRNA Extraction, BLASTN Identification, and Phylogenetic Analysis

Objective: extract representative 16S rRNA sequences from Flye, Raven, and Shasta assemblies, identify their closest species via BLASTN, and build a phylogenetic tree.

Environment Setup

conda create -y -n ssu_env -c bioconda barrnap bedtools seqkit blast mafft trimal iqtree entrez-direct
conda activate ssu_env

Tool Roles

  • barrnap: predicts 5S/16S/23S rRNA genes.
  • bedtools: extracts FASTA sequences using genomic coordinates.
  • seqkit: sorts and filters FASTA/FASTQ files.
  • BLAST+: performs remote searches against NCBI databases.
  • MAFFT, trimAl, IQ-TREE: alignment, trimming, and tree construction.
  • Entrez Direct: fetches reference sequences.

Detection & Extraction

Interpretation & Key Insight

This comparative analysis highlights distinct assembler behavior. Flye generated the most contiguous genome (N50 ? 3.77 Mb, two contigs), near-complete k-mer recovery (99.85% by KAT), and high BUSCO completeness (98.4% with no duplication), supported by a clean circular graph. Raven matched Flye?s N50 but produced 28 contigs and exhibited a BUSCO duplication rate of 44.4%, suggesting unresolved repeats despite excellent k-mer completeness (99.90%). Shasta was most fragmented (N50 ? 437 kb, BUSCO 78.2%, KAT completeness 88.41%) yet shared the same top 16S BLASTN hit as Flye (Acinetobacter baumannii, ~100% identity). Raven?s divergence (Klebsiella pneumoniae) despite strong completeness underscores that single-gene taxonomic inference can be skewed by assembly artifacts. Highly conserved loci can remain intact even in fragmented assemblies when coverage is sufficient, so integrating QUAST, KAT, BUSCO, Bandage, BLASTN, and phylogeny is essential.

Key insight: Shasta?Flye convergence on 16S identity, despite Shasta?s lower completeness, shows that conserved loci may survive fragmentation, whereas Raven?s duplication can mislead locus-specific inference. Genome-wide metrics and single-locus analyses must therefore be interpreted together.

Prokka rapidly predicts CDS, rRNAs, tRNAs, and assigns functions via curated databases. Assemblies were annotated as follows:

prokka ~/flot_project/assemblies/flye/assembly.fasta   --outdir ~/flot_project/eval/annotation/flye_prokka   --prefix flye

3. Key Output: .faa Files

  • Contain all predicted protein sequences.
  • Serve as input for KEGG?s BlastKOALA via KO assignments.
  • Enable mapping to metabolic pathways.

cp ~/flot_project/eval/annotation/flye_prokka/flye.faa /mnt/c/Users/Louis/Downloads/

5. Running BlastKOALA

Each .faa file was uploaded to KEGG?s BlastKOALA (refprok.pep database). BlastKOALA assigns KO terms and groups proteins into KEGG categories.

6. Results and Observations

  • Flye (Acinetobacter baumannii): 3,425 proteins predicted; 1,966 annotated (57.4%). Major categories include signaling, genetic information processing, carbohydrate/amino acid metabolism, and environmental processing.
  • Raven (Klebsiella pneumoniae): 6,137 proteins; 3,552 annotated (57.9%). Carbohydrate metabolism and membrane transport dominate, reflecting K. pneumoniae-specific traits.
  • Shasta (Acinetobacter baumannii): 2,984 proteins; 1,617 annotated (54.2%). Functional profile closely matches Flye despite fragmentation.

7. Integrated Project Interpretation

This final layer links structural genome data to biological function. Flye consistently produced the most contiguous and biologically coherent assembly. Shasta, though incomplete, converged with Flye on taxonomic and metabolic signatures because conserved loci were retained. Raven achieved excellent completeness numerically yet diverged in phylogeny and metabolism, underscoring that completeness alone cannot guarantee biological accuracy.

By integrating QUAST, KAT, BUSCO, Bandage, BLASTN + phylogeny, and Prokka + BlastKOALA, we obtained a holistic technical and biological perspective for each assembler.
